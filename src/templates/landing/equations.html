The U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission Regulation (NUREG-1940) titled RASCAL 4: Description of Models and Methods gave insight into how the diffusion equation can be utilized to estimate concentrations as described by Gaussian models such as TADPUFF and TADPLUME. The diffusion equations selected for use in this project originate from the TADPLUME and TADPUFF models. These models both use dispersion coefficients called sigma y and z (ùúéùë¶, ùúéùëß) to estimate the seven different EPA defined atmospheric stability classes. These coefficients are based on experimental results and are specified in the EPA Workbook of Atmospheric Dispersion Estimates.

The models are used to estimate radiological material concentrations near a point of release (TADPLUME) and a distance far from the release (TADPUFF). Two models are needed because the TADPLUME model is not accurate from a distance. As for the equations, in addition to the dispersion coefficients, other parameters needed include the radiation rate of release (Q), wind speed (Œº), off-center axis coordinates (y, x), release height (h), origin axis coordinates (x0, y0), and atmospheric mixing lid (H).
